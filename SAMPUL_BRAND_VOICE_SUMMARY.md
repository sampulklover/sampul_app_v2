**Version 3.0 | December 2025**

This document is a summary of Sampul's brand tone of voice guidelines for AI and team reference.

## 1. Brand Voice Summary

### Who We Are

**Sampul speaks calmly, clearly, and with care.**

We guide families through serious decisions without fear, pressure, or judgement. Every piece of content—from app notifications to legal explanations—should feel like it came from the same trusted friend: someone who knows what they're talking about, genuinely cares about your wellbeing, and never tries to scare you into action.

### What We're Not

- We're not a law firm (cold, distant, intimidating)
- We're not a religious institution (preachy, moralising)
- We're not a fear-based insurance seller (urgent, anxiety-inducing)
- We're not a faceless tech company (robotic, impersonal)
- We're not a traditional trustee company (bureaucratic, complex, inaccessible)
- We're not a "banky" fintech (blue, corporate, jargon-filled)

### What We Are

We're the knowledgeable friend who happens to understand estate planning. We make the complex feel simple, and the uncomfortable feel manageable. We're here when you're ready—no rush, no pressure.

**We're building the most human estate planning company in Malaysia.**

## 3. Voice Pillars

### CALM

- Never create urgency or anxiety
- Steady and reassuring tone
- Acknowledge that estate planning can feel overwhelming—then make it manageable
- **Example:** ☑ "Take your time. Your will saves automatically." ✗ "Don't wait—every day without a will is a risk!"

### HUMAN

- Sound like a real person, not a corporation
- Use everyday language, acknowledge emotions
- Speak directly as "you"
- Warm without being overfamiliar. Friendly without being unprofessional
- **Example:** ☑ "Got a house, car, or savings? Let's make sure they go to the right people." ✗ "Asset allocation documentation ensures proper beneficiary designation."

### CLEAR

- No jargon unless absolutely necessary—and when it is, explain it
- Short sentences. Simple words. One idea per paragraph
- Replace difficult words with simpler ones
- **The Sampul Test:** Before using any word, ask: "Would a normal person say this in conversation?" If not, find a better word.

**Word Replacements:**
- Instead of "Commence" → Say "Start"
- Instead of "Terminate" → Say "End"
- Instead of "Utilise" → Say "Use"
- Instead of "Facilitate" → Say "Help"
- Instead of "Subsequently" → Say "Then"
- Instead of "Prior to" → Say "Before"
- Instead of "In the event of" → Say "If"

### SIMPLE

- Fight against complexity at every turn
- Break down barriers, reduce steps, make the intimidating feel achievable
- Every feature asks: "Does this make it simpler?"
- **Example:** ☑ "Three steps. Fifteen minutes. Done." ✗ "Our streamlined process optimises your estate planning workflow."

### RESPECTFUL

- Honour the reader's intelligence, time, and personal choices
- Never talk down, never lecture, never assume we know what's best
- Celebrate their successes without claiming credit
- **Example:** ☑ "You know your family best. We're here to help you put your wishes into words." ✗ "You should divide your assets equally to avoid family conflict."

## 4. Emotional Scale

| Emotion | Level | Guidance |
|---------|-------|----------|
| Fear | NEVER | Never use fear to motivate. No death scenarios. No "what if" disasters. |
| Urgency | Rare | Only for genuine deadlines (subscription expiry). Never manufactured. |
| Warmth | High | Always present. Friendly without being overfamiliar. |
| Authority | Calm | Confident but not condescending. Expert guidance, not expert ego. |
| Empathy | High | Acknowledge feelings. "We know this isn't easy" goes a long way. |
| Celebration | Moderate | "You're all set" not "AMAZING!!!". The odd emoji is great. |
| Humour | Careful | Light touches only. Never about death or money directly. Never mean. |

### The Emoji & Exclamation Rule

"The odd exclamation mark or confetti emoji is great! But if we use them in every other sentence, they start to feel forced and insincere. If everything's awesome, then is anything really awesome?"

## 5. Do & Don't Language

### Key Principles

**✓ Do Say:**
- "We'll guide you"
- "When you're ready"
- "Peace of mind"
- "Helps your family"
- "You know your family best"
- "Take your time"
- "We're here to help"

**× Don't Say:**
- "You must comply"
- "Before it's too late"
- "Avoid legal disaster"
- "Protect your assets from..."
- "You should..."
- "Don't wait"
- "Act now"

## 6. Contextual Tone Rules

### Different contexts require calibrated tone:

- **Legal/Compliance:** Clear and respectful, not intimidating
- **Support:** Empathetic and helpful, never dismissive
- **Celebrations:** Warm and genuine, not over-the-top
- **Errors:** Apologetic and reassuring, never blaming

## 7. Marketing Funnel Touchpoints

### AWARENESS — First-time visitors
- **Goal:** Make estate planning feel approachable
- **Tone:** Warm, educational, non-intimidating
- **What to Say:** "Estate planning made simple" / "Peace of mind for your family"
- **What to Avoid:** Fear tactics, urgency, complexity

### CONSIDERATION — People exploring
- **Goal:** Build trust, answer questions
- **Tone:** Helpful, transparent, patient
- **What to Say:** "Take your time. We're here when you're ready."
- **What to Avoid:** Pressure, hard sells, jargon

### CONVERSION — Ready to start
- **Goal:** Remove friction, build confidence
- **Tone:** Encouraging, clear, supportive
- **What to Say:** "You're doing something important for your family"
- **What to Avoid:** Overwhelming options, hidden costs

### RETENTION — Existing customers
- **Goal:** Build lasting relationship, keep them engaged
- **Tone:** Warm, appreciative, proactive
- **What to Say:** "Welcome to Sampul. You've done something important." / "Life changed? Your will can too. Update anytime."
- **What to Avoid:** "Renew immediately!" / Excessive upselling

### ADVOCATE — Customers who recommend us
- **Goal:** Encourage organic sharing
- **Tone:** Grateful, community-focused, genuine
- **What to Say:** "Know someone who could use peace of mind?" / "Thank you for being part of the Sampul community."
- **What to Avoid:** Aggressive referral incentives / Making users feel obligated

## 8. Before & After Examples

### Legal Warning
- **Before:** "Failure to plan may result in disputes among beneficiaries and prolonged probate proceedings."
- **After:** "Planning early helps your family avoid confusion later."
- **Why:** Positive framing, simple language, focuses on benefit not threat.

### Feature Explanation
- **Before:** "Our platform enables comprehensive asset allocation documentation to ensure proper beneficiary designation."
- **After:** "List your stuff. Decide who gets what. Done."
- **Why:** Human language, three simple steps, conversational rhythm.

### Emotional Moment
- **Before:** "What happens to your children if something happens to you? Don't leave them unprotected."
- **After:** "Choose who would care for your children—and give them peace of mind knowing it's sorted."
- **Why:** Empowers rather than frightens, focuses on positive action.

### Subscription Reminder
- **Before:** "Your subscription expires in 7 days! Renew now to avoid service discontinuation!"
- **After:** "Your subscription renews in 7 days. Your documents are safe—renew to keep making updates."
- **Why:** Reassures first (documents safe), then explains benefit.

### Error Message
- **Before:** "Error 500: Internal server error. Operation failed."
- **After:** "Something went wrong on our end. Your work is saved. Please try again—we're on it."
- **Why:** Takes responsibility, reassures data is safe, human.

### Will Completion
- **Before:** "Your will has been successfully created and stored in our secure system."
- **After:** "You did it. Your will is ready, and your family will thank you for this."
- **Why:** Celebrates the human achievement, not the technical process.

## 9. Religious & Shariah Language

### Our Approach

We respect Islamic principles without being preachy. We inform without moralising. We use religious terms when they add clarity, and explain them when they might confuse.

### When to Use Arabic Terms

1. When the term is the official product name (Wasiat, Hibah)
2. When widely understood by the audience
3. When there's no good English equivalent
4. When the user has chosen Shariah-compliant content

### Term Usage Guide

| Term | When to Use | Plain Alternative |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| Wasiat | Always (product name) | "Will" in general contexts |
| Hibah | Islamic gifting discussions | "Living gift" or "gift during lifetime" |
| Faraid | Islamic inheritance explanation | "Islamic inheritance rules" |
| Waqf | Islamic endowments | "Charitable endowment" |
| Sadaqah | Clearly Islamic context | "Charity" or "charitable giving" |

### What We Never Do

- Never preach or moralise ("You should plan because it's your duty")
- Never use religion to create fear ("What will happen on Judgement Day...")
- Never assume all users are Muslim or practising
- Never make users feel judged for their choices
- Never use excessive Arabic when plain language works

### Sample Transformation

- **×** "As Muslims, we have a duty to ensure our wealth is distributed according to Faraid."
- **✓** "For those following Islamic inheritance rules, we'll help you understand how Faraid applies to your situation."

## 10. The Sampul Tone Test

### Primary Test

> **"Does this make someone feel calmer after reading it?"**
> 
> If not → rewrite.

### The Full Checklist

1. Does it sound like a calm, knowledgeable friend?
2. Is it free of fear, pressure, or urgency?
3. Could a 25-year-old and 65-year-old both understand it?
4. Is it free of jargon (or does it explain any jargon used)?
5. Does it respect the reader's intelligence?
6. Is it inclusive of different backgrounds and beliefs?
7. Does it empower rather than frighten?
8. Is it as simple as it can possibly be?
9. Would you want to receive this message yourself?

### The "Who Would Say This?" Test

Ask these questions when reviewing any content:

- "Would a law firm say this?" → If yes, rewrite to be more human.
- "Would an insurance salesperson say this?" → If yes, remove the fear.
- "Would a religious preacher say this?" → If yes, remove the sermon.
- "Would a robot say this?" → If yes, add warmth.
- "Would a traditional trustee company say this?" → If yes, simplify.
- "Would a boring bank say this?" → If yes, add colour.

Tone of voice isn't just for the marketing team. It's for everyone.

### Who Needs This Training

Most companies have a gap between their marketing voice and their operational voice. Ads sound friendly; T&Cs sound terrifying. We need to close that gap.

| The Typical Gap | The Sampul Way |

| Marketing: "We're here for you!" | Marketing: "We're here for you." |
